Project Goals

Our primary goal is to provide a quantitative and comparative analysis of the market strategies for Starbucks and Dunkin in the United States. We will focus on their geospatial distribution and correlation with local median household incomes.

Objective 1: Geospatial Analysis and Regional Disparity

We will describe and map the distribution of Dunkin and Starbucks stores across the U.S. to define their extent of regional dominance. We will do this by

  • Creating choropleth maps to visualize store density by region
  • Quantitativley comparing the Northeast vs. Other Regions, East vs. West, and North vs. South in order to document the concentration of Dunkin's in the Northeast and Starbucks in the West
  • Exploring whether the historical founding locations of each chain are significant factors in the current store distribution

Objective 2: Economic Correlation Model

We will train a logistic regression model to statistically analyze the relationship between local economic demographics and the probability of chain domination. We will do this by

  • Determining the strength and direction of correlation between median household income and the probability of a city having a Starbucks or Dunkin store
  • Test the hypothesis that Starbucks store location are more strongly correlated with higher median household income estimates compared to Dunkin' locations after controlling extraneous variables like population size.

Our Primary Research Questions

  1. Does the region of historical origin, Massechusetes for Dunkin and Washington for Starbucks, significantly affect the current geospatial density of stores?

  2. Does a city's median household income correlate with the presence of Starbucks and Dunkins?

  3. Which chain shows greater market saturation in its non-founding regions?

Project Datasets

Dunkin Dataset

This first dataset is from Kaggle.com, by Collin Smith. It is a CSV containing U.S. Dunkin Store Locations and has been last updated 3 years ago. Information was retrieved from Dunkin's location page at https://locations.dunkindonuts.com/en and was gathered with python using the Requests and BeautifulSoup libraries. Lat/Long data was retrieved using the Geopy library. Collin states that the NaNs in the Latitude and Longitude columns indicate the geocoder couldn't locate them. Each observation in this table is a different U.S. Dunkin store with data in variables such as location, hours of operation, and store features. In addition to the locations and hours of operation variables there is an int64 datatype row_id which is our index, and multiple boolean type variables such as drive_thru, mobile_order, dunkin_card, kcup, curbside-pickup,and has_baskin_robbins. Then the loc_lat and loc_long variables are of type float64. All other variables like address, city, state, zip, phone, and hours are object and category datatypes. Overall this table has 20 columns and 9248 rows. We plan to use this dataset to answer 3 of our main questions: Regional disparities in store locations between Dunkin and Starbucks in the Northeast/Elsewhere, North/South, and East/West. We will tidy this dataset to focus on the nominal and numerical data of locations and quantity of stores. But we hope the boolean variables such as curbside_pickup can give us insight on marketing strategies when answering future questions.

# Aggregate the raw store location data to get a single count per city/state pair

# Group Dunkin' data by the standardized 'city_name' and 'state_name' and count stores
dunkin_counts = final_dunkin.groupby(['city_name', 'state_name']).size().reset_index(name='Dunkin_Count')

# Do same for Starbucks stores
starbucks_counts = final_starbucks_df.groupby(['city_name', 'state_name']).size().reset_index(name='Starbucks_Count')

# Merge the two store counts together on the standardized keys: 'city_name' and 'state_name'
store_counts_df = pd.merge(dunkin_counts, starbucks_counts, on=['city_name', 'state_name'], how='outer').fillna(0)

# MERGE WITH POPULATION DATA
# To correctly inner join population data with store counts, we clean and standardize the population data

# To preserve the original Dataframe, create a copy of population data to work with
population_df = final_population_df.copy() #note: when copying final pop df, the standardized column names do not carry over

# Rename columns to standardized 'city_name' and 'state_name' (redo since overidden in copy)
# City -> city_name and STNAME -> state_name
population_df.rename(columns={'City': 'city_name', 'STNAME': 'state_name'}, inplace=True)

# *IMPORTANT*
# This ensures 'state_name' column that is currently full names, can be matched to the state_abbrev dict we made above
population_df['state_name'] = (
    population_df['state_name']
    .str.strip().str.title() # Strip extra white space, and convert to uppercase for clear formatting
    .map(state_to_abbrev) # Maps 'New York' --> 'NY'
    .str.upper() # Ensures uppercase to match with store data format
)

# Convert the population estimate of 2020 to numeric. Strings or nulls are coerced to NaN
population_df['POPESTIMATE2020'] = pd.to_numeric(population_df['POPESTIMATE2020'], errors='coerce')

# Final standardization of city names, stripping extra whitespace and ensuring uppercase
population_df['city_name'] = population_df['city_name'].astype(str).str.upper().str.strip()

# Drop NaNs after mapping to cleanup where standardization failed
population_df.dropna(subset=['city_name', 'state_name', 'POPESTIMATE2020'], inplace=True)


# INNER MERGE between store counts and the new tidied population data.
# We chose inner merge to only analyze cities that have complete store counts AND population data
city_analysis_df = pd.merge(
    store_counts_df,
    population_df,
    on=['city_name', 'state_name'],
    how='inner'
)
# Filter out records where population is too low (below 1000) so that our per capita calculation is stable
city_analysis_df = city_analysis_df[city_analysis_df['POPESTIMATE2020'] >= 1000]

# CALCULATE STORE DENSITY PER CAPITA (per 10,000 residents)
# Now we want to normalize store counts by population to create an accurate strore density metric for our y-axis

# Define the scale factor (per 10,000 residents)
POP_SCALE = 10000
# Calculate store density for each chain: store count/ population count
city_analysis_df['Dunkin_Density'] = (city_analysis_df['Dunkin_Count'] / city_analysis_df['POPESTIMATE2020']) * POP_SCALE
city_analysis_df['Starbucks_Density'] = (city_analysis_df['Starbucks_Count'] / city_analysis_df['POPESTIMATE2020']) * POP_SCALE

# CATEGORIZE CITIES INTO POPULATION BINS (very small, small, medium, large, very large)

# Define bins based on usual urban classification and choose labels
# Chosen bins are [0-10k], [10k-50k], [50k-250k], [250k-1M] and [1M+]
bins = [0, 10000, 50000, 250000, 1000000, np.inf]
labels = ['1k - 10k (Very Small)', '10k - 50k (Small)', '50k - 250k (Medium)', '250k - 1M (Large)', '1M+ (Very Large)']

# Use pd.cut() to assigne the population estimate into the defined bins above
city_analysis_df['Population_Bin'] = pd.cut(
    city_analysis_df['POPESTIMATE2020'],
    bins=bins,
    labels=labels,
    right=False, # Setting right=False makes the left bin inclusive and the right bin exclusive
    ordered=True # ordered=True preserves the order of catergories for aggregation and plotting
)

# FINAL AGGREGATION: AVERAGE DENSITY PER BIN

# Group the data using .groupby() with the new Population_Bin
# Calculate the mean density
density_by_bin = city_analysis_df.groupby('Population_Bin', observed=True)[['Dunkin_Density', 'Starbucks_Density']].mean().reset_index()

# Restructure the dataframe to 'long' format using melt.()
